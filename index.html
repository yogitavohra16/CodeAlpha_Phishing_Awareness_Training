What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of online scam where a cybercriminal pretends to be a trusted person or organization (such as a bank, company, government agency, or even a friend) to trick you into sharing sensitive information.

Attackers often use fake emails, websites, text messages, or phone calls that look genuine and convincing.

Simple Example

"Your bank account will be blocked within 24 hours. Click here and verify your account immediately."

The email may look exactly like it came from your bank, but it was actually created by a scammer.

If you click the link and enter your username, password, or banking details, that information is stolen.

In One Sentence

Phishing is when someone pretends to be trustworthy online to trick you into sharing personal information, passwords, OTPs, or money.

Common Phishing Targets

  • 🔑 Passwords
  • 🏦 Bank Details
  • 💳 Credit/Debit Card Information
  • 📱 OTPs
  • 👤 Personal Information
  • 📧 Email Accounts

💡 Quick Safety Tip

If a message says "Act Now!", "Verify Immediately!", or "Your Account Will Be Closed!", stop and verify the sender before clicking any links.

🎭 Social Engineering Techniques

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or revealing confidential information. Instead of hacking computers, attackers hack human trust.

🎣 Phishing

Fake emails or messages designed to trick users into revealing passwords, banking information, or personal data.

🎭 Impersonation

Attackers pretend to be a manager, colleague, bank employee, or government official to gain trust.

🎁 Baiting

Criminals offer something attractive such as free software, prizes, or USB drives to trick victims into taking unsafe actions.

📞 Vishing

Voice phishing where attackers call victims pretending to be from banks, support teams, or government agencies.

📱 Smishing

SMS-based phishing messages that create urgency and contain malicious links.

🤝 Pretexting

Attackers create a believable story to convince victims to reveal confidential information.

💡Remember

Social engineering attacks succeed because they exploit human emotions such as fear, trust, curiosity, urgency, and greed.

Real Email vs Phishing Email

✅ Legitimate Email

  • Uses official domain name
  • Proper spelling and grammar
  • No unnecessary urgency
  • Personalized greeting
  • Secure links

❌ Phishing Email

  • Misspelled domain name
  • Grammar mistakes
  • Creates panic or urgency
  • Generic greeting
  • Suspicious links

Example Phishing Email

From: support@paypa1.com

Subject: Your Account Will Be Closed!

Dear Customer, Your account has been suspended. Click below immediately to verify.

Spotting Fake Websites

Cybercriminals often create websites that look almost identical to real banking, shopping, or social media websites.

Their goal is to trick users into entering passwords, banking information, OTPs, or credit card details.

Warning Signs

  • ❌ Misspelled website names
  • ❌ Suspicious URLs
  • ❌ Too many popups
  • ❌ Poor design quality
  • ❌ Requests for unnecessary information
  • ❌ No HTTPS security lock

✅Legitimate Website

https://www.paypal.com

❌Fake Website

https://www.paypa1-login.com

How to Recognize a Phishing Email

📧 Suspicious Sender Address

Check the sender's email carefully. Attackers often use addresses that look similar to real companies.

Example: support@paypaI.com (uses capital I instead of lowercase l)

🚨 Urgent Language

Messages that create panic are common.

Examples: "Your account will be locked!" "Verify now!" "Immediate action required!"

🔗 Suspicious Links

Always hover over links before clicking.

Fake: www.amazon-security-login.com

Real: www.amazon.com

💰 Requests for Sensitive Data

Legitimate companies will never ask for passwords, OTPs, or credit card details via email. If an email asks you to share confidential information, verify the request through the company's official website or customer support before taking any action.

How To Protect Yourself

📧 Verify Sender

Always check the sender's email address carefully. Attackers often use addresses that look similar to legitimate companies.

🔗 Check Links

Hover over links before clicking. Make sure the URL belongs to the real website.

🔐 Use MFA

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication to add an extra layer of protection.

📱 Never Share OTPs

Banks and companies never ask for OTPs, passwords, or PINs through email or messages.

💻 Update Software

Keep browsers, operating systems, and applications updated with security patches.

🚨 Report Suspicious Emails

If you receive a phishing email, report it instead of interacting with it.

Real-World Phishing Case Studies

Real phishing attacks have affected millions of users and organizations worldwide. Learning from these incidents helps us recognize warning signs before becoming victims.

📄 Google Docs Phishing Attack (2017)

In May 2017, attackers launched a large-scale phishing campaign targeting Gmail users worldwide.

Victims received emails appearing to come from trusted contacts, inviting them to view a shared Google Document.

Clicking the link redirected users to a fake Google authorization page requesting account permissions.

Once access was granted, attackers gained account access and used compromised accounts to spread additional phishing emails.

⚠️Warning Signs: Unexpected document-sharing requests, suspicious permission prompts, and unusual account access requests.

📑Lesson Learned: Verify permissions carefully before granting access to any application.

💳 PayPal Verification Scam

Attackers frequently send fake PayPal emails claiming suspicious activity has been detected on a user's account.

The emails often create urgency using messages such as "Verify your account immediately" or "Your account has been limited."

Victims are redirected to fake PayPal login pages that closely resemble the real website.

Login credentials and banking details entered on these pages are stolen.

⚠️Warning Signs: Urgent language, suspicious links, spelling mistakes, and requests for sensitive information.

📑Lesson Learned: Open PayPal directly from your browser instead of clicking links in emails.

🏢 Microsoft 365 Credential Theft

Many organizations have been targeted through phishing emails pretending to come from Microsoft or internal IT teams.

Employees receive messages about payroll updates, invoices, security alerts, or shared documents.

Victims are directed to fake Microsoft login pages designed to steal usernames and passwords.

Stolen credentials are then used to access company emails, cloud storage, and confidential business information.

⚠️Warning Signs: Unexpected login requests, unfamiliar URLs, and urgent account verification notices.

📑Lesson Learned: Always verify website URLs before entering credentials.

🎬 Netflix Subscription Scam

Cybercriminals target Netflix users by sending fake subscription and payment update emails.

Messages claim payment has failed or that the account will be suspended.

Victims are instructed to update billing information through links leading to fake Netflix websites.

Credit card details entered on these pages are stolen and used for fraudulent transactions.

⚠️Warning Signs: Urgent payment requests, suspicious URLs, and requests for card information through email links.

📑Lesson Learned: Always access Netflix directly through its official website.

🔍 Common Pattern In Every Case

Attackers create urgency, fear, trust, or curiosity to make victims act quickly without verifying the source. Always pause, verify, and think before clicking any links or sharing information.
